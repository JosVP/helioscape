## DLC — THREE SYSTEM OVERVIEW

Post-V1 expansion. Three star systems, each with a distinct identity and purpose. A lot of base game engine/tech reuses — DLC is primarily new content with a few new gameplay systems. Time moves faster in DLC: the "time is an investment" emotional arc was established in the base game. Culture events are sparser and more concentrated. Player arrives with full tech and knowledge base.

Communication lag between systems is a feature: each system runs semi-independently, with transmissions arriving on lag timescales. Player makes decisions for one system, sends instructions to another, waits for results from the third. Natural rhythm of attention, no system demands constant focus.

**Narrative arc across all three systems:** Binary = applying and refining knowledge under new constraints. BHS = experimenting with what life and civilisation can become. Archive = looking back at what we were. Beginning → becoming → remembering.

## DLC 1 — BINARY STAR SYSTEM (The Challenge System)

Player applies full base-game knowledge under different physical constraints. Variable irradiance: when both stars are on the same side of the planet, hotter; opposite sides, more even heating but complex day/night cycle. Terraforming timelines shift based on stellar orbital configuration. Habitable zone is narrower and less stable than single-star systems. Player discovers base-game assumptions don't quite hold.

**Twist world idea:** one planet already partially terraformed by natural processes — not by humans. The challenge is managing a world that is *almost* right rather than transforming from scratch. Different skill, same systems.

Goal: terraform new planets and colonise them successfully. Familiar loop, harder constraints.

## DLC 2 — BLACK HOLE SYSTEM / BHS (The Alien System)

A stellar-mass black hole (~10 solar masses) fed continuously with star-lifted or redirected matter. The **accretion disk** is the light and heat source — not Hawking radiation (negligible at this scale), not the Penrose process (no visible output). The accretion disk is actively fed and therefore requires ongoing matter input to maintain luminosity. Stop feeding it and it dims over decades. This is the BHS's ongoing management layer — unlike a star, it is not self-sustaining.

### What you see from a planet's surface
Not a clean solar disk. A gravitationally-lensed accretion ring: bright on the side moving toward you (Doppler boosting), dimmer on the retreating side, dark centre (event horizon shadow). Light from the back of the disk wraps around the black hole and is visible simultaneously with the front. First-generation colonists find this deeply unsettling. Children born there find it normal. That generational shift is a culture event series.

Light colour: blue-white to yellow-white depending on feeding rate. Harder, bluer spectrum than Sol. Biologically: vitamin D synthesis requires supplementation or atmospheric filtering for UV excess. Circadian rhythms manageable with artificial lighting supplements. Shadows are soft and directionally complex — the accretion ring subtends a significant angle in the sky, producing multiple partial shadows from different parts of the ring. Artists on this world develop entirely different techniques for representing light.

### Orbital distances and gravity
At 3-4 AU from a 10-solar-mass black hole, gravitational pull is comparable to Earth's solar experience. Tidal forces at this distance are negligible — the danger zone is within ~0.1 AU. Planets are structurally safe at habitable distances. Solar system map interpretation approach holds up at these distances — same orrery display, different central object.

### The Black Forest ecosystem (planet-wide adaptation to accretion disk light)
Under blue/UV-dominant spectrum, green is an inefficient pigment. Plants maximise absorption from the violet/blue spectrum:
- **Colouration:** jet black or deep purple foliage
- **UV protection:** metallic or iridescent sheens (beetle-wing structure) reflecting excess UV that would otherwise damage DNA
- **Growth rhythm:** pulsed growth cycles driven by the accretion disk's natural flickering (variable luminosity on timescales from minutes to hours based on feeding rate variability) rather than a stable day/night cycle

Insects: accretion disk light is heavily polarised by intense magnetic fields. Insects evolve super-navigation via polarisation — millimeter-precision location awareness from the sky. Flowers evolve intricate UV patterns (neon landing strips to insects, plain black blobs to unaugmented humans).

Animals: if the accretion disk's light is too steady, circadian rhythms weaken. Animals may instead evolve rhythms based on the disk's flickering — the "sun" varies in brightness irregularly, creating a biological clock based on luminosity pulses rather than a 24-hour cycle.

**Soundscape:** organisms that evolved under variable electromagnetic radiation and strong magnetic fields communicate more heavily through sound and vibration than colour. Electromagnetic signals are less reliable carriers in this environment. The fauna of a black hole world is extraordinarily vocal — complex songs, echolocation, seismic ground communication. The first culture event from the surface notes that the night side is deafeningly loud.

**X-ray shielding is mandatory infrastructure.** Accretion disks are significant X-ray sources. A strong planetary magnetosphere is not optional — it is existential. Magnetic shield failure doesn't just threaten colonists, it threatens the entire pollinator population, which cascades to food chain collapse. This is the BHS equivalent of the L1 magnetic umbrella — permanent, critical, maintained via the infrastructure health system.

### BHS Planet 1 — Human Habitat
Standard habitable world under alien light. Design principle: don't fight the black hole environment, design with it. Black-purple vegetation, UV-pattern gardens, architecture oriented around the accretion disk's position rather than a compass rose. Culture events: what does home mean when home looks nothing like Earth? Third-generation colonists who have never seen a yellow star. Their art. Their mythology about the black hole they call their sun.

### BHS Planet 2 — Fauna and Flora Only (No Permanent Human Presence)
Seeded using the biological composition interface. Humans withdraw after ecosystem stabilises. Orbital observation continues — player watches the world change from space, sees species ranges shifting, sees unexpected developments in the ecosystem. May intervene or may not. The world evolves beyond what was designed.

First culture event after withdrawal notes something unexpected that emerged within fifty years. Not threatening — just surprising. Life doing something nobody predicted. Honest about the limits of the biological composition interface: you designed the starting conditions, not the outcome.

This world is the ethical question made physical: we engineered an entire biosphere and then left. Is it ours? Is it theirs?

### BHS Planet 3 — Extreme Seasonal Variability (Elliptical Orbit)
Elliptical orbit rather than near-circular. Closest approach (periapsis) = intense accretion disk radiation, brief. Furthest point (apoapsis) = significantly reduced radiation, prolonged. Planet rushes through bright season quickly, spends more time in the dim cool part of its orbit.

Ecosystem adaptations: rapid-growth strategies during bright season, deep dormancy or slow metabolism during the long dim period. Mass simultaneous flowering and reproduction across entire ecosystem during bright season — everything happening at once because the energy window is brief. Dim season: quiet, slow, only cold-metabolism organisms visibly active, accretion disk a faint smear rather than a dominant presence in the sky.

Vignette contrast between seasons is the most dramatic in the entire DLC: same location, completely different worlds.

## DLC 3 — ARCHIVE SYSTEM (The Emotional Payoff)

No terraforming for humanity's benefit. No colonisation goals. This system is retrospective — looking back at what Earth was, what was lost, what deserves to be remembered. Emotionally the opposite of the Binary System's challenge loop.

### Archive System Planet 1 — The Sanctuary
De-extincted species given an entire world. Earth-evolved organisms belong here, not in the alien BHS environment.

Player chooses one de-extinction category at founding (or all three on separate continents, creating temporal layers that never naturally coexisted):

- **Recent losses (last ~500 years):** dodo, passenger pigeon, thylacine, Steller's sea cow, great auk. Directly human-caused. Good DNA availability from museum specimens.
- **Megafauna losses (10,000–50,000 years ago):** woolly mammoth, cave lion, Irish elk, woolly rhinoceros, short-faced bear, giant ground sloth. Ancient DNA exists but degraded — requires gap-filling. Mammoth reconstruction already underway in real 2024.
- **Deep time (millions to hundreds of millions of years ago):** non-avian dinosaurs, Megalodon, Cambrian organisms. No usable DNA — reconstruction from fossil morphology and evolutionary modelling. What arrives is an approximation. Culture event is honest: *"We called them dinosaurs. Whether they would have recognised themselves by that name is a question we cannot answer. We built our best understanding of what they were, gave it a heartbeat, and let it go. The rest is theirs."*

The dinosaur world is the most memorable moment in the entire game including the base game. Not because of gameplay — because of what it means. A civilisation that can terraform planets and build Dyson swarms deciding to spend that power on bringing back Tyrannosaurus rex is a statement about what we value. The culture event when the first dinosaurs are confirmed self-sustaining should be very short. Just the fact of it.

### Archive System Planet 2 — The Ocean World (Europa / Enceladus Life)
If Europa life was discovered in the base game (Hunt for Life arc), this world is where those organisms are given an entire ocean planet with access to accretion disk or stellar light — an energy source they have never had in billions of years of subsurface evolution. Not a copy: chemotrophs that evolved in cold dark hydrothermal environments have never experienced photosynthesis. A sun-lit ocean changes their energy budget completely. What they become is unknown. Withdrawal after seeding, observation only.

If Europa was crashed for Venus Path C, the player uses Enceladus life instead — or this world becomes a purely speculative seeding using engineered approximations of what subsurface ocean life probably looked like. Different emotional weight depending on the base game playthrough.

### Archive System Planet 3 — The Archive World
An entire planet as a physical record of humanity's history, built for an audience that does not yet exist. Not a museum for humans — a message to whatever intelligence exists in this universe in five billion years.

What this looks like physically: mountain ranges carved into geometric patterns encoding binary data visible from orbit. Mineral veins arranged to preserve chemical records of Earth's biosphere. Monumental structures built to survive geological timescales, not human ones. The Voyager golden record scaled to a planet.

Gameplay: player selects what to encode — categories drawn from their actual playthrough. Which Mars terraforming path did they choose? Encoded in the geology. Which culture events did they flag in the history book? Carved into cliff faces in a language designed to be decodable by any sufficiently advanced intelligence. The Europa decision, whatever it was, is here. The first rain on Mars is here. Red Season is here.

No mechanics after designation. No management. The planet simply exists, permanent, carrying its record forward into time.

**Closing image of the full game including all DLC:** the Archive World, from orbit, at night. Geometric mountain ranges visible from space, encoding everything. No humans present. Cut to black. No score. No summary. Just that.

## DLC SCOPE NOTE
Three systems is ambitious but justified: most base game engine and systems reuse directly. New content is primarily: new planet visuals and vignettes, new culture events, BHS-specific ecosystem mechanics (X-ray shielding as mandatory infrastructure, accretion disk feeding management), elliptical orbit seasonal system for BHS Planet 3, de-extinction category selection for Sanctuary, archive encoding selection for Archive World. Binary System reuses almost all existing mechanics with modified parameters.

Recommend releasing as separate DLC packs rather than one bundle: Binary System first (most mechanically familiar, lowest production cost), BHS second (highest visual and content investment), Archive System third (lowest gameplay complexity, highest emotional payoff — the right note to end on).
