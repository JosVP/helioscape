# HELIOSCAPE — PLUTO DECISION
## Appendix to Terraforming Sub-GDD v4.
## Post-V1 content. Optional. Outer ring. Requires Fusion Drive.

*Caveman-compressed.*

---

## OVERVIEW

Pluto was the ninth planet for 76 years. Demoted to dwarf planet in 2006. Largely forgotten. New Horizons (2015) revealed it is genuinely beautiful — nitrogen ice plains, water ice mountains, and Tombaugh Regio: a vast heart-shaped region of lighter ice visible on its surface. Publicly beloved again, then set aside again.

This decision asks the player: does Pluto belong where it is, or does it belong with us?

No resource justification exists for this choice. It is purely a civilisational statement. The game should not pretend otherwise.

---

## THE CHOICE

**NATURALIST — Leave Pluto**
Pluto stays in the outer solar system. The solar system as it is. Some things should not be moved. An observatory CE chain: scientists study Pluto from afar, naming its features, writing about what it meant to the people who grew up calling it a planet. A quiet acknowledgement that preservation is also a choice.

CE tone: *"We decided some things are more beautiful from a distance."*

**ARCHITECT — Move Pluto to Venus**
Pluto and Charon are redirected via gravity assist sequence from 39 AU toward Venus. They arrive as a binary pair — two bodies orbiting each other as they orbit Venus. Venus, the planet no one wanted, receives the moon no one counted.

CE tone: *"Welcome back, Pluto."*

## PLUTO & CHARON — FACTS

| | Pluto | Charon |
|---|---|---|
| Diameter | 2,377 km | 1,212 km |
| Composition | ~70% rock, ~30% ice (nitrogen, methane, water ice) | ~55% rock, ~45% water ice |
| Notable feature | Tombaugh Regio (heart-shaped nitrogen ice plain) | Mordor Macula (dark reddish north pole region), deep canyons |
| Relationship | Mutually tidally locked | Always faces Pluto |

Pluto and Charon orbit a shared barycentre that sits between them in empty space — technically a binary dwarf planet system. They rotate and orbit in sync every ~6.4 Earth days. They are always facing each other. Moving one means moving both.

## ENGINEERING

- Distance from Sun: ~39 AU. Ten times further than Jupiter.
- No resource value justifies the energy cost. This is a Dyson-era project: possible only with mature Fusion Drive and Dyson swarm at high capacity.
- Transit timeline: decades of gravity assist sequence through outer solar system before inner system approach. Total from commit to Venus arrival: 50–100yr.
- No Mercury component queue benefit — this is not a Mercury production decision. It is a civilisational one. Resources drawn from general Dyson output reserve over the transit period.
- Pluto and Charon are moved as a unit. They are not separated.

---

## ARRIVAL AT VENUS

When Pluto and Charon settle into Venus orbit, two things happen simultaneously:

1. Venus gains its first moons in the history of the solar system. Venus has never had natural moons. This is unprecedented.
2. Tombaugh Regio faces Venus at arrival (narrative convention — the heart is the first thing Venus sees). This marks the start of the holiday.

From Venus's surface at ~400,000 km orbital distance:
- Pluto: angular size ~0.34° (slightly smaller than our Moon from Earth). Surface markings visible. Tombaugh Regio distinguishable as a lighter region.
- Charon: visible alongside Pluto as a separate body, slightly offset. Two objects visibly orbiting each other against the sky over a 6.4-day cycle.
- The binary pair slowly rotates around its shared centre — Charon swinging from one side of Pluto to the other and back across each ~6.4-day period.

## HEART DAY — THE HOLIDAY

**Rotation mechanic:**
Pluto's rotation period is ~6.4 Earth days. Tombaugh Regio cycles into view facing Venus once every ~6.4 days as Pluto rotates. This means the heart is technically visible for roughly 6 days out of every 13 throughout the year — but this is astronomical data, not culture. The heart cycling into view on unremarkable days is simply part of Venus's sky.

**The holiday:**
The day Pluto and Charon arrive at Venus — the first time Tombaugh Regio faces Venus — is Heart Day. This date, fixed on Venus's orbital calendar, is observed once per Venus year (224.7 Earth days). The holiday lasts 6 days: one full Pluto rotation, from the moment the heart faces Venus until it rotates away. The same 6-day window that recurs astronomically throughout the year is only *celebrated* at the annual anniversary date.

Why this works: the other times the heart cycles into view are ordinary days. Venusians might glance up and note it quietly. But Heart Day is the anniversary — the day Pluto arrived, the day Venus stopped being alone. That is what is celebrated.

**Holiday character:**
6 days. Observed across Venus. The heart is visible in the sky for the duration. Cultural tone: quiet, reflective, celebratory in a low-key way. Not a fireworks-and-parades event. More like a long weekend with meaning. People look up.

Children born on Heart Day have a specific cultural identity on Venus. It is considered auspicious. There is ongoing debate among Venusian philosophers about whether moving Pluto was right, which resurfaces every Heart Day. The debate is never resolved. That is intentional.

## CE CHAIN

*CE 1 — Arrival: "Welcome back, Pluto"*
Trigger: Pluto and Charon settle into Venus orbit. Quiet narrator. A scientist's log entry, or a child asking why the new moon has a heart on it, and someone trying to explain what Pluto used to be. Tone: bittersweet, warm. No triumph. Just arrival.

*CE 2 — First Heart Day*
Trigger: first annual Heart Day after arrival. The heart faces Venus for the first time as a cultural event rather than just an astronomical one. Venusians who weren't alive when Pluto arrived see it together. Brief, human-scale moment.

*CE 3 — The Old Debate (recurring, every ~10 Venus years)*
Trigger: periodic. A culture event revisiting whether moving Pluto was the right choice. Naturalist and Architect perspectives presented without resolution. Player reads both. No choice required — just acknowledgement that the question persists.

*CE 4 — Charon's Canyons*
Trigger: first surface mapping mission to Charon. Scientists discover the canyon network is larger and deeper than New Horizons suggested. Brief scientific CE. Venus gets a second body worth studying, permanently. Charon becomes a research destination.

*CE 5 — A Venusian Name*
Trigger: ~50yr after arrival. A cultural movement proposes renaming Pluto and Charon with Venusian names — drawing from Venus's own mythological tradition. Player does not choose the names (they are given by the culture). This CE signals that Venus has absorbed Pluto into its own identity. Pluto is no longer the solar system's forgotten body. It is Venus's moon. It belongs here now.

## NATURALIST CE CHAIN (if Pluto left in place)

*CE 1 — The Observatory*
Trigger: late game, after Venus terraforming complete. A Venusian observatory points its instruments outward to Pluto — now visible as a faint disc from Venus with sufficiently advanced optics. Tombaugh Regio distinguishable. Scientists name features from Venus.

*CE 2 — The Choice We Made*
Trigger: some years after The Observatory. A philosopher's essay or a colonist's recorded reflection. The decision to leave Pluto where it was is examined. Not regret — just consideration. The solar system has a shape. They chose not to change it. That is also a legacy.

## NOTES

- No resource value. Do not add one retroactively. The absence of justification is part of the point.
- Post-V1 only. Does not affect any gameplay balance or resource flow.
- Venus identity: Heart Day is Venus-specific. It does not exist on Mars. It is one of the things that makes Venus different from Mars culturally — its own calendar, its own sky, its own story.
- Pluto orbit circularisation: same Always Available upgrade as Jovian moon captures. Optional late investment to smooth out elliptical orbit. Not required for the holiday.
- Architect tag: not because moving Pluto is superior. Because it reflects a philosophy: the solar system is ours to shape.
- Naturalist tag: not because leaving it is passive. Because it reflects a different philosophy: some things should remain as they are.
